Only pay the AI when it's actually needed

The program reads Thai restaurant reviews and decides: is this person being sarcastic? Someone writing “great service!” when they mean the opposite.

Asking OpenAI costs money every single time. So, can a free program handle the easy ones, and only pay for the hard ones?

Yes. Paying for only 4 sentences out of 10 gave us 95% of the quality of paying for all 10. Paying for the other 6 barely helped at all.

Try the free version , paste Thai text or a link, runs in your browser, no sign-up and no API key.

The whole idea is an if-statement

A small free model reads the sentence and returns a number from 0 to 1, how sarcastic it looks.

# free, runs on your own laptop
p = model.predict(text)

if p < 0.35:
    answer = "not sarcastic"   # sure → free
elif p > 0.65:
    answer = "sarcastic"       # sure → free
else:
    answer = ask_openai(text)   # not sure → pay

That's it. The rest of the project is figuring out how big that middle gap should be, small gap means cheap but more mistakes, big gap means you're paying full price again.

The funny one: “555” is how Thai people type “hahaha”. Checking whether a sentence contains it, one line of code, no AI at all, beat every free AI model, including two built specially for Thai.

But that test was rigged (the honest ending)

Here's the catch. To build those tests we went hunting for sarcasm, so about 1 in 4 sentences in them was sarcastic. Real Thai posts are nothing like that. When we grabbed 250 completely random ones and checked by hand, only 1 in 20 (4.9%) was sarcastic.

That tiny difference wrecks everything. When sarcasm is rare, any method that “cries wolf” is wrong almost every time it shouts. So we re-scored on the realistic mix:

The one thing that saved it: the “555” trick fell from 0.59 to 0.09, and even OpenAI, left to just blurt an answer, flagged 1 in 3 sentences as sarcastic when only 1 in 20 really was. The only fix that worked was making the AI read its own confidence before deciding, the same free trick from the very first chapter. It stopped the crying-wolf and more than doubled the score, for zero extra cost. On real posts, that habit is the difference between a tool that works and one that doesn't.

Worth knowing: the realistic test had 245 sentences but only 12 sarcastic ones, so its scores show the rough level (around 0.4 to 0.5 for the best method), not an exact number. The lesson holds either way: sarcasm is far rarer, and far harder to catch, than a hand-picked test makes it look.
