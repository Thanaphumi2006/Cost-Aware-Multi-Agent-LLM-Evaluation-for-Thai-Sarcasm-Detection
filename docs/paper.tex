\begin{abstract}
Multi-agent LLM architectures (verifier pipelines, debate panels, hybrid committees) are widely
assumed to justify their extra inference cost with higher quality. We test that assumption on a task
where surface form and intent diverge sharply: sarcasm detection in Thai (\emph{prachot}).
On a 127-item human-labeled gold set drawn from product reviews and tweets, we compare five systems
under identical measurement code, tracking F1, API calls, dollar cost, and latency simultaneously.
A two-agent screener--verifier pipeline initially appears to win (F1 0.744 vs.\ 0.690). The advantage
does not survive a fair baseline: letting the single agent read its own token-level confidence and
tuning one threshold, chosen leave-one-fold-out at zero added cost, lifts it to 0.725, after which
no multi-agent system separates from it (best $\Delta$F1 $+0.019$, 95\% CI $[-0.073, +0.113]$,
paired bootstrap). Model choice dominates architecture choice: a model $6\times$ cheaper than the
flagship stays within a 0.05 F1 band, making a cheap single agent statistically indistinguishable
from a flagship pipeline at one-eleventh the cost. We further establish a \$0 floor: three
independent free methods (a 3-character regex, an embedding kNN, and a fine-tuned Thai transformer)
all converge at F1 $\approx$ 0.59, and show a three-way confidence router that retains 95\% of
flagship quality at 40\% of the cost. Expanding the gold set $2.4\times$ (302 items, 67 positive)
with adversarially plausible negatives narrows the pipeline-vs-tuned-baseline confidence interval
threefold and preserves the null result, while collapsing precision for every architecture equally:
hard negatives are a property of the data that additional agents do not repair. We conclude that for
subjective, low-resource classification, the first questions are not architectural: exhaust the
information one agent already returns, then choose the cheapest sufficient model.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Multi-agent LLM systems promise quality through redundancy: multiple calls, multiple roles, a final
adjudication. The promise is easy to believe and expensive to hold; every added agent multiplies API
cost and latency. Whether the quality materializes is an empirical question, and one that is rarely
asked with the cost axis in view.

We ask it on Thai sarcasm detection. Sarcasm (\emph{prachot}) is a deliberately adversarial target:
the writer states the opposite of their intent, so surface cues mislead exactly the models that rely
on them. Thai adds a low-resource setting, informal orthography (vowel elongations, the laughter
numeral ``555''), and, in our source domains of restaurant reviews and tweets, a culture of
exuberant sincere praise that is easy to mistake for irony. If multi-agent deliberation helps
anywhere, a task defined by hidden intent should be favorable ground.

Our contributions:
(1) a human-labeled Thai sarcasm gold set with a sharp operational rubric (sarcasm requires
\emph{feigned praise}), documented provenance and bias notes, plus a $2.4\times$-expanded hard
variant labeled blind through a purpose-built tool;
(2) a five-system, four-axis comparison (F1, calls, cost, latency) under one measurement harness,
with paired bootstrap and McNemar tests throughout;
(3) a negative result with an identified mechanism: multi-agent gains over a
\emph{confidence-aware} single agent are statistically unresolvable while costing 1.8--7.4$\times$,
because half of the apparent gain was the baseline discarding confidence it had already paid for;
(4) cost-axis anchors: a \$0 floor at F1 $\approx$ 0.59 reached independently by three free methods,
and a confidence router giving 95\% of flagship quality at 40\% of its cost.

\section{Task and Data}
\textbf{Definition.} An item is sarcastic (label 1) iff the author feigns praise or thanks while
intending the opposite. The rubric resolves the three boundary cases that dominate disagreement:
direct complaints are 0 (negative content without pretense), balanced reviews are 0 (genuine praise
plus genuine criticism), and exuberant sincere praise is 0. Undecidable items (context-dependent
intent) are excluded rather than guessed.

\textbf{Gold set.} 72{,}865 texts from Wongnai restaurant reviews and the Wisesight social-media
corpus were keyword-scored for sarcasm suspicion; 400 sampled items were pre-labeled by GPT-4o and
then human-reviewed \emph{blind} (the annotator decides before seeing the model's draft). Agreement
analysis showed the LLM over-flags sarcasm: of 17 LLM-positive items in the first review pass, the
human kept 3, motivating blind review as a design rule. Iterative mining of high-suspicion texts
brought the canonical set to \textbf{127 items (30 sarcastic / 97 not)}. Provenance caveats (some
positives were mined with the same model family later evaluated, likely inflating its recall) are
documented and affect all systems equally.

\textbf{Hard expansion.} A later blind-labeling campaign over the remaining mined candidates added
175 items, giving \textbf{gold\_v2: 302 items (67 sarcastic, 22\%)}. Because candidates were
pre-selected to \emph{look} sarcastic and ${\sim}58\%$ were judged sincere, gold\_v2 is deliberately
enriched with plausible negatives: a hard set whose absolute scores are not comparable to the
canonical set (\S\ref{sec:repl}).

\section{Systems}
All LLM systems use the same detector prompt and JSON-constrained output on GPT-4o unless stated;
prices are July-2026 list prices.
\textbf{S1 Single agent}: one call per item, argmax label.
\textbf{S1$+\tau$ Threshold}: identical calls, but the positive-class probability is read from token
logprobs and thresholded; $\tau$ is chosen leave-one-fold-out (stratified 5-fold, seed 42), so no
item influences the threshold that judges it.
\textbf{S2 Pipeline}: a screener flags candidates; a \emph{constrained} verifier may only overturn
$1\to0$ and only when confident, preserving the screener's recall.
\textbf{S3 Debate}: prosecutor and defender argue; a judge decides freely (3 calls/item).
\textbf{S4 Hybrid}: a four-agent committee mixing the above roles.
\textbf{S5 WangchanBERTa}: a Thai transformer fine-tuned on the gold set under 5-fold
cross-validation $\times$ 3 seeds (every prediction from a model that never saw the item), run
locally at \$0.

\section{Evaluation Protocol}
Accuracy is not reported: with 76\% negatives, constant rejection scores 0.76. All systems predict
the same items, so comparisons are paired: F1 differences carry 95\% CIs from a paired bootstrap
(5{,}000 resamples of the item set), and label flips are counted with McNemar's test. Costs are
computed from measured token usage; latency is wall-clock per item (p50).

\subsection{The fair baseline erases the multi-agent win}
Against the plain single agent, the pipeline's $+0.054$ looks meaningful (95\% CI $[-0.002,
+0.113]$, P(not better) = 3\%). But that baseline throws away the model's own confidence. Reading it
back, at zero extra calls and zero extra dollars, lifts the single agent to 0.725, and against
\emph{that} baseline every multi-agent system is unresolved: pipeline $+0.019$ $[-0.073, +0.113]$ at
$1.8\times$ cost; hybrid $-0.025$ $[-0.117, +0.071]$ at $4.3\times$; debate $-0.030$ $[-0.158,
+0.096]$ at $7.4\times$. Roughly half of the apparent multi-agent gain was information the baseline
had already paid for and discarded.

\subsection{Architecture: constraints beat head-count}
Why does the two-agent pipeline outperform the three-agent debate ($\Delta$ $+0.049$, at a quarter
of debate's price)? The detector starts from recall 1.000; what remains is buying precision without
spending recall. The pipeline's verifier is \emph{structurally constrained}: it can only overturn
positive verdicts, and only when explicitly confident, so it keeps recall at 0.967 while pruning
false positives. Debate returns full freedom to the panel: its defender successfully argues away 5
true positives (recall 0.833), spending the asset the detector had secured. The number of agents
matters less than the constraint structure imposed on them.

\subsection{Model choice dominates architecture choice}
The tuned single agent across five OpenAI models spanning a $25\times$ price range stays within a
0.05 F1 band, with the best score from one of the cheapest (gpt-4.1-mini: F1 0.727, \$0.015). That
cheap single agent is statistically tied with the flagship pipeline (gpt-4o, 0.744, \$0.169) at
\textbf{1/11 of the cost}. Local open models (Qwen2.5-7B, SeaLLM-7B, Typhoon-8B, OpenThaiGPT-7B,
4-bit, chat-templated, same leave-fold-out protocol) top out at 0.576: a real but modest gap of
$+0.152$ to the GPT bot, and Thai-specialized models show no advantage over general Qwen.

\subsection{Anchoring the cost axis}
\textbf{The \$0 floor.} Three independent free methods (the regex ``555'' alone, F1 0.590; an e5
embedding kNN, 0.588; fine-tuned WangchanBERTa at the default threshold, 0.590) converge at
$\approx 0.59$. The floor is a property of the task, not of any one method.
\textbf{A useful middle.} A three-way router (confident-negative / uncertain $\to$ escalate to GPT /
confident-positive) at a 40\% escalation budget reaches F1 0.690, 95\% of the GPT bot, for 40\% of
its cost; beyond that the curve is flat. A cautionary side-result: tuning WangchanBERTa's threshold
leave-fold-out \emph{underperforms} not tuning at all (0.556 vs.\ 0.590) at this $n$; at small
sample sizes, a tunable knob is a liability, and the 0.071 ``gain'' from tuning on the full set is
leakage.

On gold\_v2 the pipeline-vs-baseline difference is $+0.017$ with 95\% CI $[-0.013, +0.047]$: an
interval \textbf{three times tighter} than the original study's, still crossing zero, at
$2.55\times$ the cost. The null result is not a small-sample artifact. Absolute F1 halves for every
system because the added negatives are adversarially plausible (elongations, ``555'', effusive
tone, sincerely meant): false positives surge identically across architectures. Hard negatives are
a property of the data distribution that additional agents do not repair. The model is also
overconfident here: 210/302 items receive P(sarcasm) outside $[0.01, 0.99]$, leaving the threshold
little probability mass to work with (even the leaked-optimal $\tau$ reaches only 0.492).

\section{Limitations}
(1) \textbf{Scale.} 30 positives (canonical) cannot resolve ${\sim}0.02$ F1 differences; the
expansion raises power but is not i.i.d.
(2) \textbf{Provenance bias.} Some positives were mined with the model family under evaluation; the
bias is shared across systems, so paired comparisons stand, but absolute recall should be read
cautiously.
(3) \textbf{Domain transfer.} On 55 hand-labeled Pantip forum comments, precision falls
$0.68 \to 0.40$ (sincere praise flagged as sarcasm) while recall holds; deployment outside
reviews/tweets requires re-tuning on target-domain labels.
(4) \textbf{Single language/task.} Conclusions are demonstrated for Thai sarcasm; the \emph{method}
(fair confidence-aware baselines, paired statistics, cost as a first-class axis) is the
transferable part.

\section{Conclusion}
On a task chosen to favor deliberation, multi-agent LLM systems did not separate from a single agent
that simply reads its own confidence, while costing 1.8--7.4$\times$ more. The result replicated at
double the data with threefold-tighter intervals. Before adding agents, exhaust the one you have:
read its confidence, tune with leakage-free protocol, and choose the cheapest sufficient model.
Architecture is a question to ask after the information already purchased has been spent.
